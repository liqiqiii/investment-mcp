# Yield Curve Analysis for Investment Decisions

## Overview

The U.S. Treasury yield curve is one of the most powerful tools in an investor's analytical toolkit. It encodes the collective expectations of the bond market regarding economic growth, inflation, and monetary policy. This guide provides a detailed framework for interpreting yield curve dynamics and applying them to investment decisions, with specific attention to cyclical sectors like shipping and energy.

---

## Yield Curve Shapes and Their Meaning

### Normal (Upward Sloping)

A normal yield curve slopes upward — longer maturities yield more than shorter ones. This reflects:
- **Time premium**: Investors demand higher compensation for locking up money longer
- **Inflation expectations**: Future inflation is expected to be higher than current
- **Growth confidence**: The economy is expected to grow, supporting credit demand

**Typical normal curve characteristics:**
- 2Y yield: near the Fed Funds Rate
- 10Y yield: 100–250 basis points above 2Y
- 30Y yield: 20–50 basis points above 10Y

**Investment implication**: Risk-on environment. Favor cyclical sectors, value stocks, and higher-beta shipping/energy names. Credit spreads typically tight.

### Flat

A flat yield curve shows minimal difference between short-term and long-term yields. This signals:
- **Transition**: The economy is shifting from expansion to slowdown (or vice versa)
- **Policy uncertainty**: Markets are unsure whether the Fed will tighten further or begin easing
- **Compressed term premium**: Low demand for duration compensation

**Typical flat curve characteristics:**
- 10Y-2Y spread: 0–50 basis points
- All maturities yielding within a narrow range

**Investment implication**: Caution warranted. Reduce cyclical exposure. Begin building positions in defensive sectors. Watch closely for either steepening (recovery signal) or inversion (recession signal).

### Inverted

An inverted yield curve occurs when short-term yields exceed long-term yields. This is a significant warning signal:
- **Recession expectation**: The market expects the Fed will need to cut rates in the future due to economic weakness
- **Flight to duration**: Investors buy long bonds for safety, pushing long yields down
- **Tight monetary policy**: Short rates elevated by aggressive Fed tightening

**Typical inverted curve characteristics:**
- 2Y yield: above 10Y yield (negative 10Y-2Y spread)
- 3-month yield: above 10Y yield
- Long end anchored by recession and rate cut expectations

**Investment implication**: Strongly defensive. The 10Y-2Y inversion has preceded every U.S. recession since the 1960s. Reduce exposure to cyclical sectors. Consider defensive positions, cash, or Treasury exposure. However, the timing from inversion to recession is variable (6–24 months), and equities often rally during the initial inversion period before eventually declining.

### Steep

A steep yield curve has a large spread between short and long maturities. This typically occurs:
- **Early in economic recovery**: Fed holds short rates low while growth/inflation expectations rise
- **After aggressive easing**: Short rates near zero, long rates reflect recovery expectations
- **Reflationary environment**: Rising inflation expectations steepen the long end

**Typical steep curve characteristics:**
- 10Y-2Y spread: > 200 basis points
- Fed Funds Rate near zero or well below neutral
- Long rates rising on growth/inflation expectations

**Investment implication**: Strongly bullish for cyclicals. This is typically the best environment for shipping, energy, and commodity stocks. Bank stocks also benefit. Buy cyclicals aggressively during steep curve periods.

## Spread Analysis

### 10Y-2Y Spread (The Classic Indicator)

The difference between 10-year and 2-year Treasury yields is the most widely watched yield curve spread.

#### Historical Signal Record

| Inversion Period | Recession Start | Lead Time |
|-----------------|-----------------|-----------|
| August 1978 | January 1980 | ~17 months |
| September 1980 | July 1981 | ~10 months |
| January 1989 | July 1990 | ~18 months |
| May 1998 | March 2001 | ~34 months |
| December 2005 | December 2007 | ~24 months |
| August 2019 | February 2020 | ~6 months |
| July 2022 | TBD | Extended inversion |

**Key observations:**
- The lead time varies widely (6–34 months), making timing difficult
- Every recession was preceded by an inversion, but not every inversion leads to an immediate recession
- The **un-inversion** (steepening back to positive) often occurs closer to the actual recession start, as the Fed begins cutting rates

#### How to Use the 10Y-2Y Spread

1. **Inversion onset**: Signal to begin reducing cyclical exposure over the following 6–12 months
2. **Depth of inversion**: Deeper inversion (more negative) = stronger recession signal
3. **Duration of inversion**: Longer-sustained inversion = higher confidence in recession
4. **Re-steepening after inversion**: Often signals recession is imminent or beginning; the Fed is cutting or expected to cut soon

### 10Y-3M Spread

The spread between the 10-year yield and the 3-month T-bill rate is considered by many economists (and the New York Fed) as the most reliable recession predictor.

#### Advantages over 10Y-2Y
- The 3-month rate is more directly tied to current Fed policy (less market speculation)
- The New York Fed's recession probability model is based on this spread
- Historically, has fewer false positives

#### New York Fed Recession Probability Model

The NY Fed publishes a monthly probability of recession 12 months ahead based on the 10Y-3M spread. Key thresholds:
- **< 10%**: Low recession probability; normal conditions
- **10–30%**: Elevated caution; spread likely compressing
- **30–50%**: Significant risk; defensive positioning recommended
- **> 50%**: High probability; recession likely within 12 months

### Other Useful Spreads

| Spread | What It Captures |
|--------|-----------------|
| **30Y-10Y** | Long-end steepness; inflation expectations and term premium |
| **10Y-Fed Funds** | Monetary policy stance relative to long-term expectations |
| **5Y-3M** | Medium-term economic outlook |
| **30Y-2Y** | Full curve steepness; broadest growth/policy assessment |

## Term Premium

### What Is Term Premium?

The **term premium** is the extra yield investors demand for holding longer-duration bonds instead of rolling over short-term bonds. It is not directly observable and must be estimated by models.

### Components of Long-Term Yields

```
10Y Yield = Expected average short-term rate over 10 years + Term Premium
```

- **Expected rate path**: What the market thinks the Fed will do with short rates
- **Term premium**: Compensation for uncertainty, inflation risk, and duration risk

### Why Term Premium Matters

- **Positive term premium** (normal): Investors demand extra compensation for duration; healthy market functioning
- **Zero or negative term premium**: Distorted by central bank bond purchases (QE), flight to safety, or pension/insurance demand for duration
- **Rising term premium**: Long-term yields rise even if rate expectations are stable; tightens financial conditions; negative for risk assets
- **Falling term premium**: Supports bond prices; can mask underlying economic weakness

### Term Premium Models

- **ACM Model (Adrian, Crump, Moench)**: Published by the NY Fed; most widely referenced
- **Kim-Wright Model**: Published by the Fed Board
- Both provide decomposition of yields into expected rates and term premium

### Investment Implications

- **Rising term premium**: Long-duration assets underperform; prefer short-duration bonds and floating-rate exposure
- **Falling term premium**: Long-duration bonds outperform; growth stocks benefit from lower discount rates
- **Negative term premium**: Unusual; driven by structural demand for safe assets; can persist but represents fragility

## Historical Predictive Power

### The Yield Curve's Track Record

The yield curve's recession-forecasting record is strong but imperfect:

**Strengths:**
- Has preceded every U.S. recession since at least the 1960s
- Aggregates thousands of market participants' forward-looking views
- Cannot be easily manipulated (deep, liquid market)
- Works across different monetary policy regimes

**Limitations:**
- Variable lead time (6–24 months) makes precise timing difficult
- False positives have occurred (brief inversions without recession)
- QE and central bank balance sheet policies may distort the signal
- The "this time is different" debate recurs every cycle
- Global factors (foreign buying of Treasuries) can affect curve shape

### What Happens After Inversion?

Based on historical precedent, the typical sequence is:

1. **Inversion begins**: Stock market often continues rising (6–18 months)
2. **Inversion deepens**: Credit spreads begin widening; defensive sectors outperform
3. **Curve re-steepens**: Fed begins cutting; recession typically starts around this point
4. **Aggressive steepening**: Full recession underway; maximum rate cuts; cyclicals bottom

**Critical insight**: The best time to buy cyclicals (including shipping stocks) is typically during the **re-steepening phase**, when the curve normalizes after an inversion. This coincides with maximum pessimism and rate troughs.

## Yield Curve Effects on Equity Valuations

### The Discount Rate Channel

Equity valuations are fundamentally driven by the present value of future cash flows:

```
Stock Price = Σ [Future Cash Flow / (1 + Discount Rate)^t]
```

The discount rate incorporates the risk-free rate (Treasuries) plus an equity risk premium. When long-term yields rise:
- **Discount rate increases** → present value of future cash flows decreases → P/E multiples compress
- **Growth stocks** (long-duration cash flows) are most sensitive
- **Value stocks** (near-term cash flows) are less affected

### Sector Rotation Based on Yield Curve

| Yield Curve Phase | Favored Sectors | Underperforming Sectors |
|-------------------|----------------|------------------------|
| **Steepening (early cycle)** | Financials, industrials, cyclicals, shipping | Utilities, staples, healthcare |
| **Flat (mid-to-late cycle)** | Quality growth, healthcare, staples | Financials, deep cyclicals |
| **Inverted (late cycle)** | Utilities, staples, treasuries, cash | Cyclicals, shipping, small caps |
| **Re-steepening (recession→recovery)** | Deep value, beaten-down cyclicals | Momentum growth, previous winners |

### Shipping and Energy Sector Sensitivity

Shipping and energy stocks are **high-beta cyclicals** with strong sensitivity to yield curve dynamics:

- **Bull steepener** (long rates rise, short rates stable): Very bullish — signals growth acceleration, rising commodity demand
- **Bear flattener** (short rates rise, long rates stable): Cautious — tightening policy may slow economy
- **Bull flattener** (long rates fall, short rates stable): Mildly bearish — growth expectations declining
- **Bear steepener** (short rates fall, long rates rise): Initially bullish — Fed easing supports recovery; watch for inflation concerns

## Practical Guide to Using Yield Data

### Daily Monitoring Checklist

1. **Check the 10Y-2Y spread**: Is it widening, narrowing, or stable?
2. **Note the 10Y level**: Rising or falling? Approaching key levels?
3. **Compare 2Y to Fed Funds**: Is the market pricing rate hikes or cuts?
4. **Review breakeven inflation rates**: 5Y and 10Y TIPS breakevens for inflation expectations
5. **Check the MOVE index**: Treasury volatility — high volatility signals macro uncertainty

### Investment Timing Framework

#### When to Increase Cyclical Exposure (Shipping/Energy)
- 10Y-2Y spread is **rising from deeply negative levels** (re-steepening)
- 2Y yield is **falling faster than 10Y** (rate cuts being priced)
- Fed is **beginning an easing cycle**
- Yield curve has been inverted for **12+ months** and is starting to normalize
- Term premium is **rising** (demand for inflation protection = reflationary expectation)

#### When to Reduce Cyclical Exposure
- 10Y-2Y spread is **declining toward zero** from positive territory (flattening)
- Fed is **aggressively hiking** (2Y yield rising rapidly)
- Term premium is **falling** (flight to safety in long bonds)
- Credit spreads are **widening simultaneously** (risk-off confirmation)

#### When to Be Neutral
- Yield curve is **moderately positive** (50–150bp 10Y-2Y spread)
- Fed is on **hold** or making incremental adjustments
- No strong directional signals from term premium or breakevens

### Yield Curve and Dividend Stocks

Shipping companies often pay variable dividends tied to earnings. Yield curve analysis helps assess dividend sustainability:

- **Steep curve + strong economy**: Dividends likely to increase (growing earnings)
- **Flat curve**: Dividend growth slowing; watch for earnings pressure
- **Inverted curve**: Dividend cuts likely in 6–18 months as recession hits earnings
- **Re-steepening**: Dividends at trough; recovery ahead — accumulate for dividend recovery

### Real Yields vs Nominal Yields

**Real yield** = Nominal yield − Inflation expectations (breakevens)

- **Positive real yields**: Restrictive monetary conditions; cash and bonds competitive with equities
- **Negative real yields**: Accommodative; pushes investors into risk assets (equities, commodities)
- **Rising real yields**: Headwind for gold, growth stocks, and long-duration assets
- **Falling real yields**: Tailwind for gold, growth stocks, and risk assets broadly

## Summary: Yield Curve Decision Matrix

| Signal | Action for Cyclicals (Shipping/Energy) | Confidence |
|--------|---------------------------------------|------------|
| Curve steepening from normal to steep | **Overweight** cyclicals | High |
| Curve flattening toward zero | **Reduce** cyclical exposure gradually | Medium |
| Curve inverts (10Y-2Y < 0) | **Underweight** cyclicals; build defense | High |
| Inversion deepens and persists | **Maintain** defensive positioning | High |
| Curve re-steepens after inversion | **Aggressively overweight** cyclicals — the best entry point | Very High |
| Volatile whipsaws, no clear trend | **Neutral** positioning; wait for clarity | Low |

The yield curve is not a crystal ball, but it is one of the most reliable macro indicators available. Combined with other macro data (PMI, credit spreads, labor market), it provides a robust framework for timing cyclical investments in shipping and energy sectors.
